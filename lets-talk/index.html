Why Philadelphia Spring Water?

Philadelphians love Philadelphia more than any other city hates us. And that loyalty translates to what we buy.

Hell. We even say "water" differently than everyone. It's "wooder" here, and we're proud of it.

Philadelphia consumers show strong brand affinity for local products vs. national brands[3]. We support our own—from Wawa to Yuengling to the Eagles. It's not just preference; it's identity.

Brand loyalty to the region is our competitive moat.

We source from Greater Philadelphia. Canned here. Sold here. That resonates with Philadelphians in a way Dasani never will.

42% of Philadelphia residents already use bottled water[1]. The March 2023 Delaware River chemical spill[2] accelerated this shift. Water trust issues are real, but they're not why we'll win.

We'll win because Philadelphia wants Philadelphia water. Not Colorado mountain water. Not filtered municipal piped through who-knows-what. Our water.

We're not competing with corporate water. We're building a brand that belongs here.

Why This Model Wins

Low Carbon Footprint

Hyperlocal production = minimal shipping/waste/costs. No water trucked across the country or across the seas, stolen from disadvantaged areas (fuck you, Nestle).

High Brand Value

Local pride creates pricing power national brands can't touch. Regional identity = defensible moat.

Aluminum > Plastic

43% recycling rate for aluminum vs. 20% for plastic[5]. Premium perception built into the packaging. 71% recycled content in cans vs 3-10% in plastic.

Philadelphia Tax Advantage

Pure water is exempt from Philadelphia's 1.5¢/oz beverage tax[6]. Sweetened beverages saw 50% sales decline post-tax. We're on the right side of that shift.

Craft Beer Playbook

Yuengling. Shiner. Narragansett. They won on regional pride, not national distribution. Same playbook applies to water.

Liquid Death Proved the Market

$333 million revenue in 2024. $1.4 billion valuation[4]. First cans launched in Philadelphia, January 2019.

They own "attitude." We own "place."
